Old Linford - the 'old town' - was really less of a town than it was a burned out, grown over, rotting, decrepit husk of what might've passed for a town a long, long time ago - and even then, it would've been stretching the definition of the word.

But even if it wasn't really a town - just a collection of maybe twenty crumbling shapes of houses - it sure as shit was old.

The 'old town' was hidden away behind a corroded, rusted gate (more of an iron pipe over the road, to be honest) stretching across a backroad of a backroad, past a dozen abandoned fields gone to fallow and a dozen more still in use, past the neighborhoods and shops and schools of Linford proper, tucked into the background far, far away from the modern, respectable, well-kept core of the town. Old Linford had burnt to the ground some two hundred years ago for some reason or another (depending on who you asked: a forest fire gone awry, a livestock-lantern conflict, an attack from bandits, an intentional act of arson, "because little boys didn't brush their teeth before going to bed," or as local superstition told, the Witch's revenge, a story which was still retold and celebrated in the annual summer festival), prompting the inhabitants' exodus to Linford's current location on the other side of the Payeta Lake. Through-travelers would've had to dig deep to find any mention of the ruins outside of local folklore, and farther still to find any mention of their location; to the residents of the town, however, the exact set of directions needed to get to the gate and the wooded remains that lay beyond was common knowledge. It was just one of those things that everyone knew - it was the town's haunted house and wholly unsupervised burnout hangout all in one, and everyone had either visited at some point, or was eagerly looking forward to visiting when they got up the nerve.

Legally, of course, the town was completely, absolutely, one hundred percent off limits - condemned for as long as anyone could remember - and trespassing was punishable by jail time, or at least a stern talking to. But in practice, that meant about as much as the "gate" that swung open with a stiff breeze.

No, the town was a popular destination for teenagers looking for a secluded place to smoke, drink, or fuck, and most of Linford's populace over the age of fifteen had been there at one point or another (either in their current youth or in their long-past glory days). Tom and Jess were notable exceptions: Jess, because she was a Good Girl who didn't drink or fuck (though a very, very occasional smoke wasn't out of the question, if one of the girls had a lighter after practice and was willing to share a cig), and Tom because he was kind of a weenie, and tended not to Go Places very often unless Jess dragged him with.

Like, for example, now.

"This is fucking stupid," Tom muttered.

Tom had been apprehensive at best when Jess declared their next destination was the haunted ghost town in the middle of the forest, but adding a fine layer of freshly birthed mud into the equation was really pushing it. The onslaught of yesterday's storm had put an early end to their excursion, and they had parted (Jess's heart a little lighter, Tom's weighed down with a creeping sense of foreboding) with a promise to regroup tomorrow, but now - for the second day in a row - Tom was awake well before any self respecting person should be during the summer, and was having just a fan-fucking-tastic time navigating the results of previous day's deluge.

On top of it all, Tom was pretty sure this whole thing was a major waste of time, but he wisely kept that thought to himself.
